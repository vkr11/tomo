## The next chapter for product careers

Six months ago, taking an IC role as a director would have been career suicide. Today, it might be your smartest move.

The rules didn't just change—they inverted. Management isn't the goal. Big Tech isn't safe. Growth companies without growth are career quicksand. And the people making "irrational" moves—taking pay cuts, stepping down levels, leaving stability—are positioning themselves to win.

In this episode, Carly and I break down four career dilemmas that look nothing like they did even six months ago. This isn't about incremental shifts. This is about recognizing that the entire game changed while you were playing it.

## Key takeaways

### 1. Be a “super IC” and get close to the work

The market is rewarding people who are directly building. AI and flattening orgs mean fewer layers and a broader scope, which flows to those closest to the product. Whatever your role — PM, engineer, designer — your career will move faster if you’re in the weeds shipping, not watching from the sidelines.

### 2. Senior IC is the premium seat now

For years, the goal was management because that’s where scope and comp sat. That’s flipped. The best companies today are leaning on senior ICs to drive big pieces of work. These roles let you learn faster, own more, and stay relevant while the craft itself is changing.

### 3. Management can quietly stall your relevance

Managing people gives you breadth, but it pulls you away from the detail. And if the way we build products is being reinvented over the next 24 months, depth matters more than ever. If you’re too far removed, you’ll wake up and realize you’re managing a playbook that no longer exists.

### 4. Taking a step back can be the smart move

Moving from a director role into an IC seat, in theory, feels like a regression. It can sting to be doing work you once delegated. But in today’s landscape, it’s often the only way to retool your skills and stay marketable. Think of it as a strategic setup: one step back now that lets you take two steps forward later.

### 5. Big Tech comp is a trap

Leaders at the Magnificent Seven are often compensated 2–3x over market rate because of stock run-ups. Walking away feels irrational, which is why most don’t. The trade off is real: more stress, rolling layoffs, and less direct building, in exchange for extraordinary compensation. Some people make that call and are fine with it, but the key is to be honest about what you’re giving up.

### 6. Ex-growth companies are quicksand

If your company hasn’t reignited growth in the last couple of years, it probably won’t. Each extra month you stay, you’re underpaid, working on legacy problems, and watching the talent pool drain. These environments can feel busy, but they rarely progress your career.

### 7. Surround yourself with exceptional coworkers

The quality of the people around you shapes your trajectory more than job title or company logo. Great peers help you create luck – they open doors, start companies, and pull you into their next project. If you look around and wouldn’t bet on your coworkers, it’s a sign you’re in the wrong room.

### 8. Aim for the “elite middle” (or go found a company)

The best long-term moves right now are the elite growth companies – near-public, founder-led, AI-forward businesses like Stripe, Rippling, or Figma. They force you to stay hands-on but still offer scale and credibility. The other lane that’s career-additive is founding. It’s a higher risk emotionally, but even “failed” founders often land in strong roles because they’ve proven they can build.
