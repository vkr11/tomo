# Deb Liu — PM Interview Principles

> Extracted from 296 posts at debliu.substack.com

---

## 1. Activation Energy — Be the Person Who Gets the Ball Rolling

**One-liner:** The hardest and most valuable part of leadership isn't keeping things running — it's overcoming the inertia of the status quo to start something new.

**Sources:** *Activation Energy: The Hidden Superpower*, *The Importance of Linchpins*

**The Insight:** Deb borrows the physics concept of activation energy: it takes more energy to get something moving than to keep it in motion. She distinguishes between people who *maintain* systems (valuable) and people who *create* new ones (transformative). Her four-step formula: (1) identify the problem you care about, (2) pick one thing you can do, (3) enlist others to be part of the solution, (4) scale and grow. She started Women in Product (30,000+ members) simply by volunteering to interview every woman PM candidate at Facebook.

**Interview Application:** When asked "Tell me about a time you drove change" or "How do you handle initiative?" emphasize the *starting* — the moment you overcame organizational inertia — not just the execution.

**Example Phrasing:** "Nobody else was going to fix the gender imbalance on our PM team, so I made one change: I volunteered to be in the interview loop for every woman candidate. That single decision eventually grew into a company-wide initiative. The hardest part wasn't running it — it was starting."

## 2. Automate, Delegate, Eliminate (ADE)

**One-liner:** Every quarter, pick one thing to stop doing — the difference between good and great is leverage, not effort.

**Sources:** *Automate, Delegate, Eliminate*, *Scaling: How to Get More Out of Every Day*

**The Insight:** From a manager who told her "pick one thing every quarter to stop doing," Deb developed her ADE framework. **Automate**: turn repeated answers into documents or posts that scale (1-to-many instead of 1-to-1). **Delegate**: accept that someone doing it at 80% of your quality is better than you doing 100% of everything. **Eliminate**: audit everything you do for one week and cut one thing entirely. The key unlock for new managers: "Five people doing your old work at 80% is leverage you can use."

**Interview Application:** When asked "How do you scale yourself?" or "Tell me about a time you were overwhelmed," show a systematic approach to leverage, not just working harder.

**Example Phrasing:** "I noticed I was having the same 15-minute call twice a week answering the same questions. I wrote it up into a living FAQ document and shared it broadly — that single automation freed up 2 hours a week and helped 10x more people."

## 3. Unfair But Valid Feedback — Navigate the Contradiction

**One-liner:** Feedback can be simultaneously unfair (you wouldn't hear it if you were someone else) and valid (it reflects how people genuinely experience you) — ignoring it because it's unfair costs you real growth.

**Sources:** *Unfair But Valid Feedback*, *Tough Love: How Hard Feedback Changed My Career*

**The Insight:** Deb separates **fairness** (would someone else get this feedback?) from **validity** (does this reflect people's actual experience of you?). She spent years dismissing feedback about being "hard to relate to" because it was unfair — men wouldn't hear it. But it was valid: people genuinely experienced her as distant, and it damaged working relationships. Her framework: distinguish intent (positive) → behavior (observable) → perception (others' experience). You can't control perception, but you can adjust behavior.

**Interview Application:** When asked "Tell me about a time you received critical feedback" or "How do you handle feedback you disagree with?" show the maturity to hold two truths simultaneously.

**Example Phrasing:** "I received feedback that I came across as dismissive in meetings. My instinct was to reject it — I knew my intent was collaborative. But I forced myself to separate fairness from validity. The perception was real, so I adjusted my behavior: I started by restating others' points before sharing my own, which shifted the perception without changing my underlying approach."

## 4. Manage Your Communications Like You Manage Your Product

**One-liner:** Half of product development is great communication, but PMs don't invest nearly as much care in their comms as in their product — this is a mistake.

**Sources:** *Manage Your Communications Like You Manage Your Product*, *PM Your Career Like You PM Your Product*

**The Insight:** Deb treats communication as a product with its own strategy. Her tactical playbook: (1) Post team updates regularly — "you'd be surprised how many people read them," (2) Send pre-reads 48 hours before key meetings, (3) Send post-meeting summaries within 24 hours documenting decisions and action items, (4) Celebrate wins publicly — create a "fix of the week" culture, (5) Build a channel strategy: audit your channels, analyze what goes where, and determine the right frequency and altitude. "Ambient awareness across the company about your projects will only benefit you."

**Interview Application:** When asked "How do you work with stakeholders?" or "How do you keep leadership aligned?" show that you treat communication as infrastructure — not a soft skill — with channels, cadences, and formats.

**Example Phrasing:** "I treated our team's communication like a product launch: weekly updates with shout-outs, pre-reads 48 hours before every exec review, and post-meeting summaries within the day. Within a quarter, our team went from invisible to being featured in the company All Hands because leadership finally had ambient awareness of our work."

## 5. The Power of the Social Contract

**One-liner:** The difference between teams that trust each other and teams that walk on eggshells is whether expectations are implicit or explicit — make the unspoken spoken.

**Sources:** *The Power of the Social Contract*, *Addressing Misaligned Incentives*

**The Insight:** Deb advocates for explicit social contracts in every working relationship: written agreements about how you'll engage, what you expect, and what's off the table. Her three principles: (1) Blunt honesty, but from a place of care ("critiques always come from a good place in our hearts"), (2) Expect that things are going badly — default to problems, not perfection, (3) Accept risk — give people permission to fail. "The number one principle: we should try our best never to be surprised."

**Interview Application:** When asked "How do you build trust with a new team?" or "How do you set expectations?" describe proactively establishing an explicit social contract rather than letting norms develop organically.

**Example Phrasing:** "On day one with my new cross-functional team, I proposed a social contract: 'We default to blunt honesty, we expect problems to surface early, and we never try to surprise each other.' Writing it down and getting everyone to opt in changed the entire dynamic of our collaboration — people started sharing bad news on day two."

## 6. Build Influence Through Giving, Not Taking

**One-liner:** Influence isn't something you do *to* others — it's something others *invite* from you because you've consistently helped them with their goals.

**Sources:** *What We Get Wrong About Influence*, *Influence Through Connection*, *The Anti-Brand Builder's Guide*

**The Insight:** Deb (and guest writer Yue Zhao) reframe influence as service: "How can I help you?" replaces "I want to influence you." Three ingredients: **Knowledgeable** (have something to give), **Accessible** (make it known you want to help), **Kind** (take responsibility for how you make others feel). For introverts: influence doesn't require large networks — 15 strong relationships per year over a 20-year career creates an enormous web of deep connections. Authority naturally follows influence, not the other way around.

**Interview Application:** When asked "How do you lead without authority?" or "How do you build relationships across functions?" frame your answer around *giving* — not persuading or advocating.

**Example Phrasing:** "I don't try to influence people directly. Instead, I ask every new partner: 'What's the hardest thing on your plate right now? Can I help?' Being genuinely useful on their priorities first earns the credibility to shape our shared direction later."
