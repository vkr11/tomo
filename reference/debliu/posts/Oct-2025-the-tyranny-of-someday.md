# The Tyranny of Someday

*What I learned about time, loss, and living with urgency*

[![](images/the-tyranny-of-someday/512e38c01794.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Cuj1!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F51b87127-f6fb-4f4a-b96f-dc94d221fa30_2000x1428.png)

I sat cross-legged on the cold hardwood floor, sorting through what was left after my mom passed. The hospital bed we’d brought into her room to make her final days more comfortable was gone. All that remained were overstuffed cardboard boxes holding the fragments of her life.

The night before she died, I had just returned from a long work trip. I sat beside her bed and crocheted a small stuffed penguin as we watched the State of the Union together. That quiet, ordinary moment was to be our last.

For years, my mom and I had said we would scan her photo albums “someday.” I finally began, fearing someday might never come. After gathering up her albums and shipping them off, we started getting her photos back, digitized. But before I had the chance to go through them with her, she went into hospice, and soon she was gone. What remained were disintegrating albums and envelopes of dusty, unlabeled photos. Now I have gigabytes of her unlabeled memories sitting in the cloud.

That moment shifted something in me. Life suddenly felt fragile, fleeting. I now appreciate just how dangerous the word “someday” really is.

### **The Math of a Life**

Oliver Burkeman, in his book *Four Thousand Weeks*, reminds us that the average human lifespan is about that long. Just over 4,000 weeks, roughly 80 years (if we are lucky).

By the time you are 18, nearly 1,000 of those weeks are already behind you. If you’re 40, half are gone. If you’re 60, you may have only 1,000 or so left.

We rarely think of our lives this way. We act as though time is an endless resource, like sand on a beach. Each week goes by feeling like any other, but it is one less block in the wall of your life.

There’s another number that startled me when I first learned it: by the time you leave home at 18, you’ve already spent more than 90 percent of the total time you will ever spend with your parents.

The family dinners, the rides to school, the everyday moments of proximity all fit within those first 18 years. After that, the time compresses into holidays, quick visits, the occasional vacation. If you’re lucky, you might see them once or twice a year, maybe a few days each time. If you’re 25 and you see your parents twice a year for five days each visit, that’s ten days a year. Over the next 40 years, that’s 400 days, just a little over a single year together, spread out over a lifetime.

That’s why, after my dad passed, I asked my mom to come live with us. A few years later, we invited my in-laws to move to our town so they could all watch their grandkids grow up. We had been shuttling back and forth to NC to see them as much as we could, but it just wasn’t enough. We chose to spend that time together, not someday, but while they still could. We lost both my in-laws just a few months before my mom passed. I am glad we didn’t wait to move them closer.

### **The Illusion of Someday**

So why do we keep deferring things in our lives? Partly, it is how we experience time. Humans see time as infinite. Yes, we all know we will die someday, but it feels infinitely far off. We have no easy way to measure something that we can’t tangibly hold.

We say things like:

* *Someday I’ll write my book.*
* *Someday I’ll take that trip.*
* *Someday I’ll start taking care of my health.*
* *Someday I’ll repair that relationship.*

Someday feels distant, like a possibility that sits out on the horizon. But someday is a lie. It isn’t a date on the calendar. It’s a trick we use to make procrastination feel okay right now.

We put off saving for retirement. We avoid dealing with a small oil leak. We don’t care for our health like we should. I learned the hard way with my mom’s photo albums. Procrastination has a cost.

### **What Really Matters**

While I was at Ancestry, I saw how future generations look back at the lives of their ancestors. Entire lives were condensed into nodes on a family tree. A name. Three dates: birth, marriage, death. If they’re lucky, a handful of documents, some photos, and maybe the place they were born and died. A lifetime in just a few words.

What doesn’t make it into those records and nodes are the dreams they had and what they had wanted out of life. Those get buried with the word “someday.”

In the first few weeks of Stanford Business School, a professor asked us to write our own obituaries. We were all fresh-faced and excited to start this chapter of our lives. But we were forced to stop and ask ourselves: *What will my life stand for?* When we returned for our fifteen-year reunion, two of our classmates were no longer with us. Their someday was ended too soon.

My mentor, Brad Smith, shared the quote from Mark Twain with me: “The two most important days in your life are the day you are born and the day you find out why.”

The tragedy is that too many of us never live our why. We postpone finding out. We assume we’ll have time later. We let “someday” rob us of today.

How do we escape the tyranny of someday?

1. **Face the math.** Accept the reality of 4,000 weeks. Count the visits left with your parents. Imagine your own countdown clock. Not to frighten yourself, but to wake up to the reality and plan accordingly.
2. **Name one thing you’ve been postponing.** A dream, a project, a conversation. Write it down. Speak it aloud. Make a plan.
3. **Take one step today.** Not tomorrow, not next month. Write a page. Make the call you have been dreading. Book the ticket to the place you want to go. Forgive someone who may not deserve it. Action breaks the spell of someday.
4. **Screen everything with your why.** Say yes to those things that align with your why and say no to the things that don’t matter. Focus on what will matter when your countdown hits zero.

### **The Liberation of Now**

Here’s the paradox. Facing the finiteness of time doesn’t need to be something to dread. It can feel freeing.

When you accept you can’t please everyone and do everything, you stop trying to. Stop trading your limited days for things that don’t matter. Stop telling yourself “later,” and start asking “why not now?”

There is a joy in living this way. Knowing that each week is one of only 4,000 makes you care for each one because they are fragile and irreplaceable.

---

When I think back to my mom’s photos, I know that someday never came.

Your story won’t be written someday. It’s being written now by the choices you make today.

You can keep living under the tyranny of someday. You can endlessly defer, endlessly wait. Or you can choose to reclaim your weeks, however many are left, and spend them with intention.

Because someday may not be coming. But today is here, and today is all we’re promised.
