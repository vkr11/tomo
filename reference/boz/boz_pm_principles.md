# Andrew Bosworth ("Boz") — PM Interview Principles

> Extracted from 138 posts at boz.com

---

## 1. Influence Over Authority

**One-liner:** Authority can be taken away, but influence — the property of people *wanting* your help — cannot.

**Sources:** *Influence Over Authority*, *Damn the Org Chart*, *Traits I Value*

**The Insight:** Boz argues that seeking authority is a cultural trap: "people who want authority generally aren't allowed to have it." True leverage comes from being someone others *choose* to work with. This requires being kind (so people feel good after working with you), being competent (telling people when you *can't* help is critical — saying yes to everything and then disappointing people is the #1 mistake), and using authority only as an absolute last resort.

**Interview Application:** When asked "Tell me about a time you led without authority" or "How do you align cross-functional teams," frame your answer around *earning* the right to influence rather than wielding positional power.

**Example Phrasing:** "I've always believed that the best way to lead is to be the person people choose to come to for help. In this case, I earned credibility by being transparent about what I didn't know, which made my recommendations carry more weight when I did have conviction."

## 2. CTFOIGT — Take Ownership When Nobody Else Will

**One-liner:** When everyone cares about a problem but nobody's prioritizing it, the opportunity belongs to whoever steps up and says "I got this."

**Sources:** *CTFOIGT*, *Ownership and Entitlement*, *Radical Responsibility*

**The Insight:** Boz's famous acronym — "Chill The F*** Out, I Got This" — captures the moment when a group identifies something important but nobody takes ownership. Ownership isn't about being territorial; it's about taking responsibility for work getting done and, critically, communicating broadly when plans change. The opposite — entitlement — is the language of "the company" or "that team" or "someone should." Owners use "we" and specific names.

**Interview Application:** When asked "Tell me about a time you showed initiative" or "Describe a time you went above and beyond," tell a story where chaos or indecision existed and you stepped in decisively — not because it was assigned, but because you recognized the vacuum.

**Example Phrasing:** "I noticed we had a 30-email thread where everyone was expressing opinions but nobody was converging. I just blocked off time, built a working prototype, and shared it. The thread went quiet and we started shipping."

## 3. Escalate Early and Often

**One-liner:** Escalation isn't a sign of failure — failing to escalate almost always is.

**Sources:** *Escalate Early and Often*, *Strong Opinions Weakly Held*, *Cycle Time*

**The Insight:** Boz argues the conventional view is "exactly incorrect." Teams that defer conflict to avoid escalation are actually *increasing* cost — people become entrenched in as little as a week. Escalation within *hours* (not days) exposes weaknesses in the strategy and improves the overall constellation of decisions. Critically, compromise itself is dangerous because "compromise makes bad products" — an uninspired middle ground satisfies nobody and teaches nothing if it fails.

**Interview Application:** When asked "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a stakeholder" or "How do you handle conflict," avoid stories where you just compromised. Show that you escalated the fundamental tension quickly and used the disagreement to sharpen the strategy.

**Example Phrasing:** "When I saw the two teams were diverging, I escalated within a day rather than trying to negotiate bilaterally. The exec review actually uncovered a gap in our strategy that neither team had seen, and the resolution improved all downstream decisions."

## 4. Communication Is The Job

**One-liner:** You cannot prevent communication — even silence sends a signal — so you must invest to do it well.

**Sources:** *Communication Is The Job*, *Listening Is The Job*, *Operations*

**The Insight:** Boz structures communication as a layered system: (1) Layer your message so people can engage at their level of interest, (2) Consider the second-order audience — the people your audience talks to afterward, (3) Communicate defensively by anticipating the most cynical interpretation, (4) Communicate early and often — "people can deal with imperfect information but they cannot stand information insecurity." The companion piece, *Listening Is The Job*, flips this: "The only way to ensure success is to take responsibility for getting the information you need."

**Interview Application:** When asked "How do you manage stakeholders?" or "Tell me about a communication challenge," show that you treat communication as a *system* you actively design, not just a soft skill you happen to have.

**Example Phrasing:** "I structure every major update in layers — a one-line TL;DR for execs, a paragraph for cross-functional leads, and a full doc for my team — because I know my audience's audience will only repeat the simplest version."

## 5. Radical Responsibility — Never Let Anyone Outflank You With Criticism

**One-liner:** Be your own biggest critic in every review; the paradox is that proactively exposing risks builds more trust than hiding them.

**Sources:** *Radical Responsibility*, *Traits I Value* (#15 Growing), *Hearing Feedback*

**The Insight:** Boz's rule: "I don't want anyone to be more critical of my work than I am." He proactively surfaces bugs, risks, and conflicts before anyone else finds them. This eliminates the antagonistic dynamic of reviews — people stop digging because they trust you're offering the full picture. As a bonus, when he does share optimism, it lands harder because the audience knows his critical lens is always on. "There is no buy-in so rich as fully informed buy-in."

**Interview Application:** When asked "Tell me about a failure" or "How do you handle feedback," show that you don't perform vulnerability — you *systematically* front-load risks in your presentations and reviews so trust compounds over time.

**Example Phrasing:** "I always open status reviews with the top 3 risks and what's behind schedule before sharing wins. My leadership knew that if I wasn't worried about something, they didn't need to be either — which earned me a lot of autonomy."

## 6. Data Humility — Beware False Precision

**One-liner:** The cost of something is always easier to measure than its benefit; don't let the data you have outweigh the data you don't.

**Sources:** *Data Downfalls*, *Focus on Impact*, *The Streetlight Effect*

**The Insight:** Boz identifies five specific data traps PMs fall into: (1) **Focalism** — over-indexing on one metric when the product is holistic, (2) **Hard to Measure Success, Easy to Measure Failure** — killing a feature because you can measure its cost but not its value, (3) **Complex Data** — building for the "average" user who doesn't exist when your data is multimodal, (4) **Analysis Paralysis** — debating a feature for longer than it would take to build, (5) **Survivorship Bias** — learning only from successes. His antidote: "the marginal value of information is below the value of immediate action."

**Interview Application:** When asked product sense or analytical questions like "How would you measure the success of X?" show that you understand the *limits* of metrics, not just which ones to track.

**Example Phrasing:** "We were stuck in analysis paralysis— debating whether the feature was 'good enough' for longer than it would have taken to build it. I reframed the question from 'how good is it?' to 'how can we make it better?'— which meant shipping first and then iterating with real data."

## 7. Mission → Strategy → Tactics (The Hierarchy of Alignment)

**One-liner:** A good strategy is always wasted by bad execution, but sometimes bad strategy is saved by good work.

**Sources:** *Mission, Strategy, and Tactics*, *Operations*, *Executive*

**The Insight:** Boz defines three layers sharply: **Mission** = what (aspirational, short, takes a stance), **Strategy** = how (the standard is high — it means "we must do this AND cannot succeed without it"), **Tactics** = speed of execution. Common failure modes include confusing vision with strategy, treating revenue as mission instead of strategic imperative, and deferring tactical decisions upward instead of empowering local leadership. His most provocative take: strategy should assume *good but not perfect* execution — if you're not executing well, you re-evaluate strategy rather than just pushing harder.

**Interview Application:** When asked "How do you set a product vision?" or "Walk me through your strategic planning process," use Boz's sharp definitions to show you think in layers and don't conflate mission with tactics.

**Example Phrasing:** "I distinguish sharply between strategy and tactics. Strategy is 'we cannot succeed at our mission without doing this' — if it doesn't clear that bar, it's tactical. This framing helped my team focus on the three things that truly mattered instead of twenty nice-to-haves."

## 8. Optimize Cycle Time, Not Process

**One-liner:** Most companies have excessive parallelization and insufficient prioritization — do less, decide on prototypes not decks, and push decisions down.

**Sources:** *Cycle Time*, *Operations*, *Failure Theory*

**The Insight:** Boz argues cycle time governs both speed and *team satisfaction*. Long cycles shift teams from proactive to reactive, which undermines conviction. His four levers: (1) Push decision-making down to strong local leaders, (2) Reduce the number of people in each loop — "a document with 50 co-authors is a very poor use of time", (3) Make decisions on demos and prototypes rather than docs and decks, (4) Do less — prioritize ruthlessly. Separately, in *Failure Theory*, he warns that adding process to prevent minor failures often increases the capacity for *catastrophic* ones, like climbers roped together on a mountain.

**Interview Application:** When asked "How do you improve execution?" or "Tell me about a time you sped up delivery," focus on structural changes to cycle time rather than "working harder."

**Example Phrasing:** "I cut our review cadence from monthly to weekly, which instantly reduced the unit by which work slipped. Then I reduced the decision loop from 12 stakeholders to 4 representatives — which made each meeting 3x faster and 5x more decisive."

## 9. Shoshin — The Expert's Curse

**One-liner:** "In the beginner's mind there are many possibilities; in the expert's mind there are few."

**Sources:** *Shoshin*, *Career Cold Start Algorithm*, *The Gift of Naïveté*

**The Insight:** After being removed from News Feed, Boz watched his replacements ignore his advice — and build something much better. The trade-offs he'd made years earlier were no longer valid, but his expertise blinded him to that reality. His personal innovation cycle was 12-18 months: after that, confidence became complacency. His countermeasures: (1) Systematically document your core assumptions and revisit them, (2) Embrace naiveté — encourage new team members to ask hard questions instead of trying to fit in, (3) His *Career Cold Start Algorithm*: spend the first 25 minutes asking "tell me everything I should know", 3 minutes on biggest challenges, 2 minutes on who else to talk to. Repeat until no new names emerge.

**Interview Application:** When asked "How do you ramp up on a new domain?" or "Tell me about a time you changed your mind," demonstrate intellectual humility and a systematic approach to challenging your own assumptions.

**Example Phrasing:** "When I joined the team, I ran my cold-start algorithm: 30-minute meetings with every stakeholder, asking three questions. By the third day, I had a map of influence that looked nothing like the org chart — and a list of quick wins the team had deprioritized for months."

## 10. Solve Demand-Side Problems

**One-liner:** We too often try to solve demand problems on the supply side — the harder path of increasing core value is the one with the best long-term outcome.

**Sources:** *Demand Side Problems*, *The Revenue Takes Care of Itself*, *Focus*

**The Insight:** In Facebook's ads business (2012-2017), Boz resisted the tempting path of growing ad *supply* (more inventory = predictable returns). Fixed demand meant you were just "buying your way down the inventory in terms of quality." Instead, he invested in the demand side — making ads more relevant and valuable — which was slower to move, harder to measure, but increased the value of *existing* inventory. His generalization: any time you're solving a problem by making more of something (features, content, inventory), ask whether the real issue is that people don't want what you already have.

**Interview Application:** When given a product strategy case like "How would you grow revenue for X?" resist the temptation to list supply-side features. Analyze the demand side first.

**Example Phrasing:** "My instinct was to add more placement options, but I realized we were solving a supply problem when the real issue was demand — advertisers weren't getting enough ROI from existing placements. We focused on relevance and targeting, which increased the value of every impression we already had."
