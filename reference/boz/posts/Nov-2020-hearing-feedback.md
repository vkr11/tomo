# Hearing Feedback

As a whole, humans seem to accept that they will die but seem to have a keen
interest in precisely how it happens. We fixate on rare events like shark
attacks and plane crashes. While those do seem like terrible ways to die, they
aren’t very common. It would be much more rational to care about the
likelihood of death than the method. But the medical establishment has to run
entire advertising campaigns just to get us to go see the doctor for routine –
but potentially life-saving — check-ups. We should worry more about heart
disease than we seem to.

We make this mistake all the time. We focus on mechanism at the expense of
substance.

The place I see this most often in my job is how people respond to
[feedback](https://boz.com/articles/feedback-is-a-gift). We care a lot about
how people give feedback to us. If someone is whiny or aggressive we give
ourselves permission to discount the substance of their feedback even though
the two are unrelated. While it is worth [guiding people towards more
effective feedback](https://boz.com/articles/be-helpful), we only hurt
ourselves and our products when we fail to find the value in even poorly
delivered critique. The best of us learn to embrace our [most ardent
critics](https://boz.com/articles/malcontents).

Once you spot this pattern you will see it everywhere. But it still takes work
to set your ego aside and listen. I really do want my wife’s advice on my
appearance. But the way I get that advice still seems to matter a great deal
to me. I am working on it.
