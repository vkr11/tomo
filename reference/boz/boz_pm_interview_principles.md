# Andrew Bosworth ("Boz") - Core PM Principles for Interviews

Andrew Bosworth’s leadership philosophy is highly applicable to Product Management interviews, particularly for behavioral rounds focusing on influence, conflict resolution, and execution. Below is a synthesized guide to his core principles, mapped to common PM interview themes.

## 1. Influence Over Authority
* **Source:** *[Influence Over Authority](https://boz.com/articles/influence-over-authority)*
* **The Principle:** Seeking authority is a trap. Authority can be taken away; influence cannot. Influence is the property of people *wanting* your help because you are competent and kind, not because they are required to take it.
* **Interview Application (Leading Without Authority):** When asked how you align teams, don't just say you "built relationships." Explain how you earned influence by **being competent** (which includes knowing when to say "no" so you don't let people down) and by treating formal authority as an absolute last resort. 

## 2. Strong Opinions, Weakly Held (The Value of Productive Tension)
* **Source:** *[Strong Opinions, Weakly Held](https://boz.com/articles/strong-opinions)*
* **The Principle:** Compromise makes bad products. A product built to appease conflicting teams usually results in an uninspired "tyranny of the averages." The better solution is to embrace disagreement and turn it into productive tension to sharpen a single, cohesive vision.
* **Interview Application (Conflict/Disagreement):** When asked about a time you disagreed with engineering or design, avoid stories where you just "compromised." Share a story where you leaned into the conflict to uncover a fundamental tension in the strategy, ultimately leading to a sharper, unified decision that teams could "disagree and commit" to.

## 3. Communication is The Job
* **Source:** *[Communication is The Job](https://boz.com/articles/communication-is-the-job)*
* **The Principle:** PMs are the connective tissue of an organization. You cannot *not* communicate; even silence sends a signal. Therefore, Communication *is* the job, not just an overhead task.
* **Interview Application (Stakeholder Management):** Use his specific communication tactics when detailing how you aligned a complex project:
  * **Layer Your Message:** Structure communication so people can engage at their level of interest (TL;DR up top, deep details below).
  * **Consider the Second-Order Audience:** Distill your strategy into short, memorable phrases so that when your stakeholders talk to *their* teams, the message doesn't mutate.
  * **Communicate Defensively:** Anticipate the most cynical interpretation of your roadmap and adjust your messaging to preempt it.

## 4. Ownership vs. Entitlement
* **Source:** *[Ownership and Entitlement](https://boz.com/articles/ownership-and-entitlement)*
* **The Principle:** Entitlement externalizes problems ("the company is broken", "that team blocked us"). Ownership internalizes them ("I didn't align them early enough"). Owners believe nothing is owed to them and that they must make their own results.
* **Interview Application (Failure/Mistakes):** When answering failure questions, be hyper-vigilant about your language. Avoid blaming "leadership changes" or "resource constraints." Instead, use the language of an owner: "I failed to keep leadership aligned with our changing risk profile, so when they shifted priorities, we were caught off guard. Here is how I fixed it..."

## 5. Optimizing Cycle Time
* **Source:** *[Cycle Time](https://boz.com/articles/cycle-times)*
* **The Principle:** The speed of execution and the satisfaction of a team are governed by "cycle time." Long cycle times force teams to be reactive rather than proactive. 
* **Interview Application (Execution/Delivery):** When asked how you speed up delivery or handle slow teams, discuss how you reduce cycle time by:
  * Pushing decision-making down to strong local leaders rather than requiring multiple "vertical leaps" up the org chart.
  * Reducing the number of people in the loop (a document with 50 co-authors has diffuse accountability).
  * Making decisions on prototypes and demos rather than just docs and decks.

## 6. Solving Demand-Side Problems
* **Source:** *[Demand Side Problems](https://boz.com/articles/demand-side)*
* **The Principle:** We too often try to solve demand problems on the supply side, which leads to unsatisfactory results and diminishing returns. 
* **Interview Application (Product Strategy/Growth):** If given a case study on how to increase revenue or engagement, don't just jump to increasing the *supply* of features, inventory, or notifications. Analyze the *demand* side. Explain that while optimizing supply is easier and gives predictable short-term ROI, improving the core value (demand) is harder but yields the best long-term outcome.

## 7. The Shift to Management (Moving Up an Abstraction Layer)
* **Source:** *[Learning to Stop Worrying and Love Management](https://boz.com/articles/learning-to-love-management)*
* **The Principle:** Transitioning from an IC to a PM Leader involves moving to a higher level of abstraction—from debugging code to debugging conversations and teams. "What was sufficient to get to this point is insufficient to get further."
* **Interview Application (Leadership/Mentorship):** When discussing your growth into a senior or group PM role, articulate this exact transition. Explain how you shifted your focus from absolute individual impact to "return on investment" by scaling through your team's output rather than your own tactical execution.
