# The Bitter Lesson: Product version

### Will Learning loops beat designed UX?

The other day I was in a product review where the team walked us through a well-crafted interface for an agent designed to boost your organizational IQ. Elegant flows, good polish, solid user value.

And yet I left uneasy because it struck me that no matter how clever the design, it wouldn’t get better the more people used it. It was static, a one-time expression of ***human*** insight, and not a system that evolves with scale.

Which reminded me of this year’s Turing Award winner Richard Sutton and his must-read essay “The.Bitter Lesson”.

### Sutton’s Bitter Lesson

Sutton’s claim, drawn from decades of AI research, was blunt: clever handcrafted systems always lose to methods that learn at scale.

For years we saw AI researchers poured human knowledge into machines using rules, heuristics and pipelines. Each wave felt ingenious, and each was eventually surpassed by brute-force methods that simply learned more.

For example, chess heuristics fell to search plus compute. Or take speech trees that were crushed by end-to-end deep networks..Hand-engineered linguistic features disappeared under transformers trained on raw text.

The bitter lesson was that human cleverness doesn’t compound as well as compute scales learning.

### Bitter Lesson: The Product Version

The same pattern I suspect can now unfold in products.

Product teams naturally jump to solve user problems with more scaffolding: extra flows, carefully crafted UIs, polished controls. The problem is that it works once but it doesn’t improve with use.

OTOH, products that behave like models, meaning that they are broad, feedback-driven and designed to learn WILL keep getting better.

### Bitter Lessons in the Making?

Where might we already be seeing today’s “handcrafted pipelines” in product design?

Here are a few hypothetical examples:

* Flight search: dozens of sliders, filters, and calendars versus simply asking, “Find me the cheapest nonstop flight from Boston to London next week that I can upgrade with miles.”
* Creative workflows like Photoshop: years of mastering layers, palettes, and adjustment sliders versus saying, “Make the sky moodier, add contrast to the face, and remove the background objects.”
* Spreadsheets and dashboards: ribbons of formulas, pivot-table builders, and endless filters versus, “Compare Q2 revenue in EMEA and North America and show it as a bar chart.”
* Calendar scheduling: grids of times and time-zone math versus, “Find the next available 45-minute slot with my leadership team before Friday and schedule it.”

All of these are beautifully engineered experiences. But if Sutton’s lesson holds, they may be the product equivalent of feature engineering and heuristics — human-crafted interfaces destined to be eclipsed by model-driven products that learn and adapt.

### Bitter Lesson Parallels Between Models and Products

1**. Tons of Compute → Intelligence on Tap**

In models, scaling compute created breakthroughs. In products, model-backed surfaces put intelligence on tap. Basically, you no longer need a feature for every use case.

**2. General Algorithms → General Interfaces**

In models, general-purpose methods outlasted task-specific pipelines. In products, general entry points like chat, canvases, or command palettes do the same, versus custom flows and GUI elements, letting intelligence stretch across domains without constant redesign.

**3. Learning → UX Designed for Learning**

In models, learning loops like gradient descent and fine-tuning drove improvement. In products, UX must capture edits, retries, and corrections as signal. Interfaces shouldn’t just deliver answers; they should help the system improve.

### **Prompt for Product builders**

Handcrafted UX, however well-designed, doesn’t scale. Learning and feedback loops do. Is there a bitter lesson in the making for product builders?

**Learning Loops » Designed UX.**
