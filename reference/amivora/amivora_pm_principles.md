# Ami Vora — PM Interview Principles

> Extracted from 78 posts at amivora.substack.com

## 1. It's Not Prioritization Until It Hurts

**One-liner:** Prioritization means cutting things that *are* valuable so you can double down on even bigger goals — if nothing on your "cut" list disappoints you, you're not cutting hard enough.

**Sources:** *It's Not Prioritization Until It Hurts*, *Execution Beats Strategy Every Time*

**The Insight:** Ami reframes prioritization away from "removing unnecessary work" (if it were unnecessary, you'd have already cut it). True prioritization requires saying yes to a few big goals and blocking time for them — rather than saying no to many small things, which just frees up scraps. "Don't say no to several small ideas; say yes to a few big ones." Whatever doesn't fit on the calendar must be delegated.

**Interview Application:** When asked "How do you prioritize?" or "Tell me about a difficult trade-off," show real pain — describe something genuinely valuable you cut, not just obvious waste.

**Example Phrasing:** "I don't think you've truly prioritized until you've cut something that made you uncomfortable. We killed a feature I was personally excited about because it competed for the same engineers as our top strategic bet. That hurt, but it's why the bet shipped on time."

## 2. Execution Beats Strategy Every Time

**One-liner:** A mediocre strategy with great execution teaches you *why* you lost, so you can iterate — great strategy with poor execution teaches nothing.

**Sources:** *Execution Beats Strategy Every Time*, *The 3 Key Jobs of a Product Manager*

**The Insight:** Ami learned at Meta that a culture focused on execution gives you "so many more tries." Poor execution on a perfect strategy wastes time *and* learning — you can't tell whether the loss was strategic or operational. Her formula: allocate 20/80 on strategy vs. execution, create a tight customer feedback loop, and create incentives that celebrate improvement over grand planning.

**Interview Application:** When asked "What's more important, strategy or execution?" avoid the diplomatic "both." Take Ami's sharper stance: execution first, because it's the engine that iterates toward the right strategy.

**Example Phrasing:** "At my last company, we tried three different approaches to the same ad format before one stuck. The key wasn't having the perfect strategy — it was having an execution machine that could ship a version, learn from it, and ship the next version within weeks."

## 3. If I Can See a Problem, It's Mine to Solve

**One-liner:** If you're close enough to see a problem, you're probably best positioned to solve it — don't wait for someone to tap you on the shoulder.

**Sources:** *If I Can See a Problem, It's Mine to Solve*, *Leadership = Taking Accountability for Things No One Is Asking You to Do*

**The Insight:** Ami spent years waiting for an omniscient group of senior leaders to hand her the most important problems. She eventually realized: "What if no one else can even see it?" Her hack when entering a new area: ask "What are you doing today that I can take off your plate?" This creates immediate exposure to the most important work. She frames leadership at the senior level as: there's no mold, no playbook — you take accountability for what you believe matters even when nobody asks.

**Interview Application:** When asked "Tell me about a time you demonstrated leadership" or "How do you handle ambiguity?" tell a story about a problem you *chose* to own, not one you were assigned.

**Example Phrasing:** "Nobody asked me to fix the onboarding funnel — it sat between two teams and both assumed the other owned it. I drafted a proposed solution over a weekend, and both teams were relieved to have someone drive it. That's how I learned: if you can see a problem, it's yours."

## 4. The 4 Components of Product Quality

**One-liner:** Quality isn't a vague aspiration — it's four measurable streams: Performance, Bugs, Completeness, and UX Consistency.

**Sources:** *The 4 Components of Product Quality*, *Creating a Product Vision*

**The Insight:** Ami resolves the perennial team argument ("is our product high-quality?") by breaking quality into four independent, mostly measurable components — using a utility analogy (do the lights turn on?). **Performance**: speed is "unexpectedly magical." **Bugs**: random failures disproportionately erode trust. **Completeness**: supporting every part of the user's end-to-end flow. **UX Consistency**: making every room's light switch in the same place. No need for a grand unified quality metric — improve each independently.

**Interview Application:** When asked "How do you define product quality?" or "How did you improve quality on a product?" use this four-part framework to give a structured, concrete answer.

**Example Phrasing:** "I decomposed 'quality' into four streams: perf, bugs, completeness, and consistency. We tracked each independently — no grand unified metric — and let each team own improvements in parallel. Customers felt the product get better incrementally, which built trust."

## 5. Listen, Document, Decide — The Ramp-Up Framework

**One-liner:** When you're new, reverse the typical order: listen first, document obsessively, then decide — the documenting itself earns trust and builds your judgment.

**Sources:** *Learning to Make Decisions in a New Space*, *Creating a Product Vision*, *A Career Cold Start Algorithm* (referenced pattern)

**The Insight:** New leaders instinctively jump to decision-making to prove competence. Ami suggests reordering: (A) Listen — understand context and how individuals feel, (B) Document & Execute — write down what you hear, distribute notes, figure out action items, (C) Decide — only after A and B build your pattern recognition. The documenting itself is uniquely valuable: "anything written more than a few weeks ago is likely out of date, which means what I'm learning may be the best snapshot of what's really happening today."

**Interview Application:** When asked "How do you ramp up quickly?" or "Tell me about your first 90 days in a new role," describe a structured listen-first approach rather than early decision-making.

**Example Phrasing:** "In my first two weeks, I sat in on every team meeting and wrote detailed notes — not to decide anything, but to document the decision process. Those notes became the team's most up-to-date reference document, and by week three, I had enough pattern recognition to start making confident calls."

## 6. Soliciting Hard Feedback — Make It a System

**One-liner:** Feedback is a gift, but only if you build a system to receive it — identify the people whose feedback you've been subconsciously avoiding, and limit your response to "thank you" and "tell me more."

**Sources:** *Soliciting Hard Feedback*, *Dealing with Hard Feedback*, *Two Secrets That Keep Me Growing*

**The Insight:** Ami's tactical framework: (1) Identify the people whose feedback you've been avoiding — they have the toughest and most useful observations, (2) Ask questions that reframe: "If I stepped into my manager's job today, what gaps would keep me from succeeding?" (3) Seed the hardest feedback yourself: "I heard I come off as impatient — have you seen that?" (4) Keep people talking for 10 minutes — they'll use up easy feedback first and get to the tough stuff by minute 6, (5) Thank everyone and circle back to show impact.

**Interview Application:** When asked "How do you handle feedback?" or "Tell me about a time you received critical feedback," show a *system* for actively seeking it, not just gracefully receiving it.

**Example Phrasing:** "I made a list of the five people whose feedback I'd been subconsciously avoiding — they're the ones most likely to have the insight I need. I scheduled 1:1s with each and asked: 'If I had to step into my manager's role today, what would hold me back?' The pattern that emerged completely changed how I ran my meetings."

## 7. Grow Through Complexity, Autonomy, and Throughput

**One-liner:** Career growth follows a specific progression for each new problem type: first handle the complexity, then operate autonomously, then increase throughput.

**Sources:** *To Get Promoted, Get Better at Complexity, Autonomy, and Throughput*, *Turning "What a Failure" into "What I Learned"*

**The Insight:** Ami identifies three axes that distinguish senior performers: **Complexity** (how hard a problem can you solve?), **Autonomy** (can you handle it with minimal guidance?), and **Throughput** (how many hard problems can you handle simultaneously?). Critically, these develop *in order*: you first learn to tackle complex cross-functional problems with heavy guidance, then build pattern recognition to operate independently, then create systems that let you parallelize.

**Interview Application:** When asked "How have you grown as a PM?" or reflecting on your career trajectory, structure your answer along these three axes to show deliberate progression.

**Example Phrasing:** "The first time I ran a cross-functional launch, I needed daily guidance from my director. By the third, I'd built playbooks that let me run independently. By my fifth, I had systems in place to run two launches in parallel — that's the complexity-to-throughput progression that unlocked my promotion."
