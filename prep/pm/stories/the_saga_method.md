# The Saga Method

> A framework for organizing your career into Sagas, Epics, and Stories — and translating them to any behavioral interview.

---

## The Structure

The Saga Method separates **what you've done** from **what the interviewer is evaluating** — and gives you a system to connect the two in real time.

```
YOUR SIDE (Supply)                  THEIR SIDE (Demand)
──────────────────                  ────────────────────
Saga                                Question Category
  └── Epic                            (Conflict, Failure,
       └── Story                       Ambiguity, Execution...)

                                    Principle
                                      (your approach, philosophy,
                                       or operating framework)
```

**Your Side** is fixed — it's your career, organized into a hierarchy you build once and maintain.
**Their Side** changes per company and per interviewer — but your material adapts to it through Principles.

> **Is this overkill?** If you have fewer than 3 major career chapters, a flat list of stories may be all you need. This system pays off when you have enough depth that a flat list becomes hard to navigate under pressure.

> **Where this applies:** The Saga Method is designed for **behavioral interviews** ("Tell me about a time...") and **project retrospectives**. It does *not* cover case interviews, system design questions, product sense prompts, estimation questions, or coding rounds — those require different frameworks entirely.

---

# Part 1: Your Side (Supply)

Your career material, organized into three layers.

## Saga

A **Saga** is a major chapter of your career — defined by a company, team, and overarching mission arc.

- **Not** your HR job title. It's the *narrative arc* behind the title.
- One title might contain two Sagas (e.g., your mission drastically shifted mid-tenure).
- Two titles might be one Saga (e.g., you evolved from IC to TL on the same mission).

**What it captures:**
- The macro environment and stakes
- Your strategic mandate
- A 20–40 second intro pitch you'd actually say out loud

**Examples:**
> *"I was leading the Delivery & Logistics platform at a global e-commerce giant during a period of 10x hypergrowth — reinventing our routing engine to handle scale without skyrocketing delivery costs."*

> *"I was the first PM at a Series A health-tech startup, standing up the patient data platform from scratch while navigating complex international healthcare regulations."*

---

## Epic

An **Epic** is a sustained initiative within a Saga — typically spanning multiple months or quarters.

- Each Saga generally contains 2–5 Epics.
- An Epic has a clear problem, approach, cross-functional map, and outcome.
- It's the natural "zoom-in" when an interviewer wants execution detail.

**What it captures:**
- Problem and goal
- Options considered and decision rationale
- Cross-functional partners and alignment mechanisms
- Outcomes and metrics
- What you'd do differently

**Examples:**
> *"Within that logistics mission, one key workstream was the Dynamic Routing Engine — building infrastructure to optimize delivery routes in real-time based on traffic and courier availability."*

> *"One of my first epics at the health-tech startup was the Patient Consent System — creating a unified way to manage data permissions across 15 different regional jurisdictions."*

---

## Story (Keyframe)

A **Story** is a specific moment — a conflict, tradeoff, failure, or insight — inside an Epic. It's the answer to *"Tell me about a time when..."*

- Each Epic contains 3–6 Stories (we call them "keyframes").
- Stories are the atomic unit — the raw material you work with.
- A Story is a *neutral recording of what happened*. It only becomes an interview answer when you tell it through a Principle.

**What it captures:**
- A 10-second hook (bottom-line-up-front)
- The raw narrative arc of what happened, what you did, and what resulted

**Example hook:**
> *"I overrode the engineering team's proposed database architecture for the consent system after auditing our multi-region load requirements — which prevented a major outage during the European launch."*

# Part 2: Their Side (Demand)

What the interviewer is evaluating. This changes per company, but the structure is always the same: a question that probes for a competency.

## Question Categories

A **Question Category** is the *prompt shape* — the type of behavioral question being asked.

Common categories:
- **Conflict / Disagreement** — "Tell me about a time you disagreed with..."
- **Failure / Mistake** — "Tell me about a time something went wrong..."
- **Ambiguity / Strategy** — "Tell me about a time you had to make a decision without enough data..."
- **Execution / Grit** — "Tell me about a time you had to deliver under pressure..."
- **Technical Bet** — "Tell me about a time you made a technical judgment call..."
- **Leadership / Influence** — "Tell me about a time you influenced without authority..."

The Question Category tells you **which Story to grab** — but it doesn't tell you how to tell it. That's the Principle's job.

## Principles

A **Principle** is your personal philosophy, framework, or approach that you bring to a class of problems. It's your operating system.

- *"I always map the opposing team's incentives before I try to align."*
- *"When data and intuition conflict, I dig deeper into the data before overriding either."*
- *"I treat every compliance requirement as a consumer trust opportunity, not a constraint."*

Principles aren't lessons you learned after the fact. They're **the reason you made the decision you made** — and they're reusable across your entire career.

### Principles are portable. Company rubrics are plug-ins.

Your Principles are yours. They travel with you. What changes per company is the *mapping*:

| Your Principle | Amazon LP | Google Signal |
|---|---|---|
| "Map incentives before aligning" | Earn Trust | Collaboration / Googleyness |
| "Dig deeper before overriding" | Dive Deep | Analytical Rigor |
| "Compliance as opportunity" | Invent & Simplify | Strategic Thinking |

You don't rewrite your prep for each company. You translate your Principles to their language.

# Part 3: The Translation

This is where Supply meets Demand — how you take a raw Story and shape it to answer a specific question.

## The Interview Flow

```
Hear Question → Read the Room → Pick Story → Apply Principle → Deliver
```

### Step 1: Hear the Question Category
*"Tell me about a time you disagreed with a partner team."* → **Conflict**

### Step 2: The Read
Before you pick a Story, take a beat. What is the interviewer *actually* evaluating?

A Conflict question could be testing:
- **Your conviction** — did you stand your ground? (Have Backbone)
- **Your empathy** — did you understand their side? (Earn Trust)
- **Your pragmatism** — did you find a third option? (Invent & Simplify)

You won't always know for sure. But reading the room — the role, the company culture, the interviewer's body language — helps you pick the right Principle.

### Step 3: Pick a Story
From your Build View (see below), grab the Story that best fits both the Question Category and the Principle you want to showcase.

### Step 4: Apply the Principle (the Lens)

This is the core move of the Saga Method. **The same Story can be retold through a different Principle depending on the question.** The raw event stays the same. The Principle determines which details you foreground, which parts of the action you emphasize, and how you close.

**One Story, three lenses:**

| Question Asked | Principle Lens | What You Foreground | How You Close |
|---|---|---|---|
| *"Tell me about a failure"* | **Learn & Be Curious** | The initial architecture choice was wrong. How you discovered it late in the cycle. | *"What permanently changed in our testing rituals..."* |
| *"Tell me about a conflict"* | **Have Backbone** | The pushback from the Legal team on data retention. How you held your ground with user privacy data. | *"The compromise eventually met both legal and user needs..."* |
| *"Tell me about changing direction"* | **Are Right, A Lot** | The signals that told you to pivot from manual to automated underwriting. The ambiguity. | *"The data I used to convince the CEO..."* |

The Principle shapes the emphasis *and* the close. The close is what sticks with the interviewer.

### Step 5: Deliver

Use whichever delivery format feels natural:

| Format | Structure | Best For |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| **STAR** | Situation → Task → Action → Result | Structured, safe, widely understood |
| **Freytag's Pyramid** | Exposition → Rising Action → Climax → Falling Action → Resolution | Dramatic storytelling, tension and engagement |
| **DIGS** | Dramatize → Indicate alternatives → Go through what you did → Summarize impact | Leading with stakes and conflict |

## In Practice

**Question: "Tell me about a conflict with a partner team."**
1. **Saga:** *"During my time leading the Patient Data platform at a health-tech startup..."*
2. **Epic:** *"...specifically during the unified Patient Consent System work for multi-region compliance."*
3. **Story through Have Backbone lens:** *"We had a fundamental disagreement with the Legal team on data retention windows. My approach is to always understand the opposing team's incentives first..."* → [foreground: the conflict, how you held your ground, how you brought them along]

**Same Story, different question: "Tell me about a time you changed direction."**
1. **Saga:** *same*
2. **Epic:** *same*
3. **Story through Are Right, A Lot lens:** *"Midway through building the Consent System, I realized our initial architectural approach wouldn't meet strict European latency requirements. The signals were..."* → [foreground: the pivot decision, the ambiguity, the data that informed the call]

The raw event is the same. The Principle lens reshaped what you emphasized and how you closed.

# Part 4: Organizing Your Prep

## The Build View (source of truth)

This is how you organize and maintain your stories. Each Story sits inside its Epic, tagged with the Principles it can be told through:

```
SAGA: Delivery & Logistics Platform (Global E-commerce, 2021–2023)
├── Epic: Dynamic Routing Engine
│   ├── "Database Override"    → [Have Backbone] [Dive Deep] [Learn & Be Curious]
│   ├── "Peak Season Deadline" → [Deliver Results] [Bias for Action]
│   └── "Courier Incentive"    → [Are Right, A Lot] [Insist on Standards]
├── Epic: Driver Experience
│   ├── "Fleet Partnership"    → [Earn Trust] [Think Big]
│   └── "Payout Transparency"  → [Think Big] [Customer Obsession]

SAGA: Patient Data & Compliance (Health-Tech Startup, 2023–2024)
├── Epic: Patient Consent System
│   ├── "Retention Conflict"   → [Have Backbone] [Are Right, A Lot]
│   └── "Deployment Crunch"    → [Deliver Results] [Bias for Action]
└── ...
```

## The Lookup View (interview cheat sheet)

The reverse index — derived from the Build View. Glance at it 5 minutes before an interview:

```
HAVE BACKBONE; DISAGREE & COMMIT
  → "Database Override" (Routing Engine / E-commerce)
  → "Retention Conflict" (Consent System / Health-Tech)
  → "Red Team vs Launch Date" (Security / Fintech)

LEARN & BE CURIOUS
  → "Database Override" (Routing Engine / E-commerce)
  → "Architecture Deep Dive" (Consent System / Health-Tech)

ARE RIGHT, A LOT
  → "Database Override" (Routing Engine / E-commerce)
  → "Courier Incentive" (Routing Engine / E-commerce)
  → "Retention Conflict" (Consent System / Health-Tech)

DELIVER RESULTS
  → "Peak Season Deadline" (Routing Engine / E-commerce)
  → "Deployment Crunch" (Consent System / Health-Tech)
```

The same Story appears under multiple Principles — because you can tell it through different lenses.

**Coverage gaps** are immediately visible: if a Principle has only one Story under it, you're thin. If every Story comes from the same Saga, you're over-reliant.

## Getting Started

1. **List your Sagas** — Write 3–5 major career chapters. Draft a 2–3 sentence intro pitch for each.
2. **Break each Saga into Epics** — Identify 2–4 sustained initiatives per Saga. Note the problem, approach, and outcome.
3. **Mine each Epic for Stories** — Extract 3–6 keyframe moments: conflicts, tradeoffs, failures, insights.
4. **Identify your Principles** — What are the 5–10 philosophies or approaches you consistently bring to problems? Write them down in your own words.
5. **Map Principles to company rubrics** — Translate your Principles to the LP / rubric of each company you're targeting.
6. **Practice re-angling** — Take one Story and practice telling it through 2–3 different Principle lenses. Notice how the emphasis and the close shift.
7. **Build your Views** — Create the Build View (source of truth) and derive the Lookup View (cheat sheet). Look for gaps.
